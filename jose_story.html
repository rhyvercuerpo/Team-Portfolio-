Justice for María José Estupiñán: A Tragedy of Femicide in Colombia

Date: May 20, 2025

The murder of Colombian model and influencer María José Estupiñán, 22, has shocked the nation and reignited criticism of Colombia’s failure to protect women from gender-based violence. María José was killed on May 15 in Cúcuta by a suspect disguised as a delivery man. Surveillance footage shows the attacker fleeing after shooting her in the face.

Authorities revealed that María José had a history of reporting domestic violence. Just a day before her murder, a judge had ordered her abuser to pay compensation. Police suspect the killing may be a femicide — the targeted killing of a woman because of her gender.

“She was a young, enterprising woman with a whole life ahead of her, but those dreams are cut short like the dreams of many women in this country,” said Magda Victoria Acosta, president of the Colombian Judiciary’s gender commission.

Col Leonardo Capacho of Cúcuta police confirmed previous reports of abuse by her ex-partner. “It could be a case of femicide,” he stated, “but that is still under investigation.”

Women's rights groups argue this was a preventable tragedy. Alejandra Vera, director of Woman, Speak Out and Move It, emphasized systemic failure: “María José, like thousands, did what the system demands — she reported, presented evidence, and asked for help. But the state let her die.”

She highlighted Colombia’s grim statistics: a femicide every 28 hours, with most going unpunished. “There are no effective protection orders, no proper shelters, and impunity emboldens abusers,” Vera added.

The Colombian Observatory of Femicides reported 886 femicides in 2024 — the highest in seven years — with 207 already recorded by March 2025. Despite existing laws, Human Rights Watch notes that perpetrators are “rarely held accountable.”

María José’s murder is being compared to that of Mexican influencer Valeria Márquez, who was killed on livestream just days earlier. Activists now plan marches in Cúcuta and Bogotá demanding justice and reform.

At her funeral, a family member said: “María José, life for you was a wonderful journey, but very short. Fly, fly very high, Majo.”
